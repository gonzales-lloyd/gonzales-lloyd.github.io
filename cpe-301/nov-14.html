Why are critical sections necessary?

Suppose we have the following:

volatile byte count;

ISR(Timer1_OUF_vector){
	count = 10;
}

void setup(){
	//...
}

void loop(){
	count++
}

An increment can take quite a few forms in terms of low-level instructions, such as:

LDS R24, 0x200
SUBI R 24, 0xFF
			; --- What if our interrupt happens here?
STS 0x0200, R24

The solution is to disable interurpts that may occur when accessing shared variables, so we don't ned up with states that conflict with what we expect.

Therefore, we might instead have

void loop(){
	disableInterrupts(); // Turn off TIMSK# (where # = the timer number)
	count++;
	enableInterrupts(); // Turn on TIMSK
}

It is important to note that interrupts take time; internal interrupts take 2.625 nanoseconds of overhead. External interrupts take 5.125 nanoseconds. You shouldn't be putting huge sections of code in them; the intent of interrupts is to change a small set of states and do processing on those states (perhaps from some external data), not to take the place of your main functions.

FPGAs are field programmable gate arrays.

Recall that the CPU consists of a control unit and an ALU (arithmetic logic unit). The CPU handles the tasks of control and arithmetic/logical operations.

Now, the general structure of the ALU (approximately) looks like:

Now, this approach to logic is physical - it's hardware-based and final. You can't rip it out and "fix" it if it doesn't work for whatever reason; if it doesn't work, you have to throw it away.

Each CPU/ALU is designed for exactly one task. Such a hardware approach is generally more expensive, and only becomes profitable when you sell larger batches of these CPUs. It also entails long development cycles; most people can't just design CPUs at home, much less produce and distribute them.

An alternative approach is that of programmable logic. The idea here is to design a circuit with logic that can change programatically (that is, with a program). This has several advantages:

  • Mistakes are easier to fix
  • Faster development cycles
  • A single piece of hardare can have many applications

But of course, this has several disadvantages as well:

  • The circuit is more complex
  • It requiers programming knowledge to operate
  • It is much more expensive to produce; you must produce even larger batches to be profitable, assuming you want these to be profitable
