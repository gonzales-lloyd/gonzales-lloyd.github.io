We discussed a theorem regarding the DAP, but in text:

Theorem

The DAP, with men proposing, gives each man his favorite feasible outcome and each woman her least favorite feasible outcome.

The DAP, with women proposing, gives each man his least favorite feasible outcome and each woman her favorite feasible outcome.

If in both the men-proposing and the women-proposing scenarios we get the same (core) matching, there exists only one core matching (since this implies that both groups get their favorite feasible outcome in either scenario).

House-swapping game
...

In the house-swapping game, the player set consists of a set of traders . There also exists a set of houses , where .

Originally, trader owns house , trader owns house , and so on (up to trader owning house ). We assume that at any point, the traders only have use for exactly one house. (It's worth noting this generalizes to the case where traders start with no house, in which case we can assign them a "fake" house that represents the lack of a house.)

We now move to giving everyone a list of preferences:

  • A: e, a, c, d, b
    • This means trader A likes trader E's (initial) house best, their own house second-best, and so on.
  • B: c, a, e, b, d
  • C: d, a, b, c, e
  • D: d, b, c, e, a
  • E: a, b, c, d, e

How should the houses be allocated amongst the traders, where each trader gets one house? This is the binding contract in the cooperative game.

This type of game is called a one-sided matching market, because there exists only one type of trader. In contrast, the marriage game is a two-sided matching market.

Just like how in the marriage game we explored the concepts of individual rationality and blocking pairs throug arbitrary matchings, we do the same here. Consider the following allocation:

  • A-e, B-c, C-d, D-b, E-a

We're giving four of the traders their best choice, while the last (D) gets their second choice. So this allocation seems plausible, but note that D prefers their house to anybody else's - they could have just not participated in the game and come out better. So this is not individually rational for trader D.

Now, consider:

  • A-a, B-e, C-b, D-d, E-c

This is indeed, individually rational; every person does at least as well compared to them just keeping their own house. However, note that the coalition - a subset of - consisting of traders A, B, and E can "block" by swapping their own original houses in the following way:

  • B gets house a
  • A gets house e
  • E gets house b

This leads to a "cyclic" trade of houses:

where all three traders are better off than under . (Also note that A and E can form a coalition themselves.) Therefore, the coalition can block via the trading cycle described above.

Note

You may wonder why trader A doesn't get house B - after all, the arrow goes from B to A. However, the notation is that the arrowhead represents who agrees to the trade - that is, the person who gives the tail their house. In other words, B proposes to A that A gives B their house. So the net result is that B gets the house that A holds (which is house ), A gets the house that E holds (which is house ), and so on.

In other words, the arrows represent the direction in which traders propose (sort of), not the direction that the houses move in. As stated by Shapley, this trading cycle is a directed graph where an edge points from a trader to the owner of their top-ranked house.

Think of it as "A likes E('s house)" rather than "E gets A's house."

Worth noting is that the coalitions can be arbitrary large - compare this to the marriage game, where blocking "coalitions" were always pairs.

Our objective here is to find an allocatino that cannot be blocked by any coalition through a trading cycle. Note that this covers the case that the result is individually rational, since this is equivalent to stating that "there exists no one-person blocking coalition".

The core of a house-swapping game is the set of all allocations for which no coalition , taking their original houses, can swap amongst themselves such that it strictly improves the position of every member over an assigment .

Moving back to the example, consider:

  • A-e, B-c, C-a, D-d, E-b

Notice that it cannot be possible that A, B, or D can be in a blocking coalition, since they all received their first choice. (Blocking coalitions must strictly improve every member's assigment, and since there's no better option, A, B, and D cannot be members of such a coalition.)

Therefore, the only possible blocking coalitions are the coalitions consisting of C and E, C alone, and E alone.

  • C can only keep their own house, but C is getting a better house in compared to their own (relative to their own preferences).
  • E is also getting a better house in compared to their own.
  • C and E can only do two things between themselves: C and E block individually, or C and E swap between themselves. But C getting e and E getting c is worse than the result of , so this is not a blocking coalition, either.

We can verify, then, that these three coalitions do not block . Therefore, is a core allocation.

However, this isn't completely satisfactory; consider that the coalition A, E can weakly block if A gets e and E gets a. Then, A stays the same, while E improves. Here, "weak" refers to the notion that every player in the coalition is at least as well off as they were in , with at least one players strictly improving from .

This moves us to our definition of the strict core in a house swapping game. This is the set of allocations for which no coalition , taking their original houses, can swap amongst themsleves such that it strictly improves the position of at least one of its members while everybody else is no worse off over .

By definition, this means that the (set of) strict cores are a subset of the core. Then, our objective is to search for core allocations - or better yet, strict core allocations.

This gives rise to a question - do core/strict core allocations always exist? If so, how do we find them? Is there a DAP for this? It turns out that the answer is indeed, yes. The algorithm here is called the top trading cycle algorithm.
