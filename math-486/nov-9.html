This type of problem is called a linear complementarity problem, or LCP. It is much like a linear program (LP), except that we're not trying to optimize (maximize or minimze) anything; we're just looking for some and that satisfies everything, so this is an existence problem.

Additionally, notice that the constraints and are not linear - they're called "complementarity" constraints, which is how we end up with an LCP instead of an LP.

Despite this, LCPs are a generalization of LPs; that is, any LP can be written as an "equivalent" LCP. The argument for this uses the complementary slackness theorem, which is addressed in MATH 751 (and other places).

Cooperative game theory
...

In cooperative games, players can make binding contracts. This leads to more solution concepts beyond the minimax solution and Nash equilibria; before, players more or less had to take things into their own hands. But if players can work together, there are more things they can do than if they worked alone.

Our first solution concept is the TU-solution, which is used only with a special class of games - namely, two-player cooperative games. Later, we'll talk about more than two-player games.

Take the following example. Suppose two companies are selling the same product in a market. They can set either high or low prices.

highlow
high(12, 10)(4, 14)
low(20, 0)(10, 6)

(Payoffs are described in thousands of dollars.)

From a noncooperative perspective, both players have dominating strategies - that is, for both to play low, yielding a worse payoff than if both had just played high. This problem is isomorphic to the arms race, which in turn is isomorphic to the prisoners' dilemma. Due to these dominating strategies, the minimax solution and the Nash equilibrium give the same outcome - for both companies to set their prices low, yielding profits of $10,000 to both.

But suppose that the companies are permitted to cooperate. Then, suppose that both players agree to set their prices high, gaining a combined , yielding $22,000. This is better than the combined payoff of any other cell.

But for this to be a binding contract, they need to agree on how the $22,000 should be split - that is, to agree upon the payoff vector , which in general is for some . Notice that because can be anything (since the payoffs will still add up to $22,000), you can get fairly ridiculous "effective" payoffs, like the first company losing $1,000,000 from the deal and the other gaining $1,022,000.

There's also quite a few assumptions we've made here, which we will cover later.
