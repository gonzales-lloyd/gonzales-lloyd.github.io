Chapter 2

Business processes
...

Every company has business processes. A process could be defined as:

  • A flow of material
  • A flow of information
  • A flow of knowledge

Or more concisely, it's a set of activities or steps that the business takes in its day-to-day activities. These may be tied to a functional area or be cross-functional. If it's tied to a functional area, it's tied to one department and no other department touches it. But if it's cross-functional, there may be people in different areas of the business that are involved.

Ultimately, the important thing is that business processes may be assets or liabilities. Processes could be really inefficient and have too many steps, costing a company money or create products that aren't safe or aren't high-quality. But the same process could be good or bad for another company.

Functional
...

What are some examples of functional business processes, which are tied to one department (generally)? One example is assembling a product in manufacturing and production. That department doesn't ever expect to bring it to HR for assembly; it's always going to happen in a specific spot in one department.

Sales and marketing (depending on the company) will take a specific processes to idtnfy customers, documenting customers and their interests. They're not going to reach out to accounting to have them call people to see if they can convince somebody to buy a product.

Financial statements are generally done by a finance and accounting department. And depending on where you work, human resources tends to be the only department that's directly involved in hiring new employees. They may also ask for specific departments to be involved, such as when it may be beneficial for another department to use their experience to decide who's a good candidate or not. In these cases, it may be cross-functional.

Cross-functional
...

What are some examples of cross-functional processes? One is order fulfillment; sales, accounting, and manufacturing/production all have to work together to decide whether or not an order actually causes a product to be shipped each of them handles different roles in generating the order, submitting the order, checking and approving credit, make an invoice, assembling the product, and shipping the product. This is clearly cross-functional, since several departments are involved.

You can look at this and figure out where an information system can be used to automate or eliminate changes. This is where business processes and systems are tied together, since you're able to look at a processes and decide where you might want to make it more efficient.

The role of IS in business processes
...

Information systems enhance business processes by:

  • Increasing the efficiency of existing processes, automating steps that were previously manual
  • Enabling netirly new processes, possibly changing the flow of information (perhaps in the past, you had to deliver papers by hand and sign them, which could be replaced with something like DocuSign) or replacing sequential steps with parallel steps (enabling things to happen at the same time instead of waiting on things)

Types of information systems
...

Information systems are used to increase and expedite the flow of information. Note that not every company, again, is Amazon or Google – they may just be looking for some simple software that improves their processes in some way.

One potential system you may see is an intranet, which is an internal company website that is accessible only by employees. An extranet is a company website that's accessible externally only to vendors or suppliers (not everybody).

Another is a set of tools for celebration is teamwork. These are pieces of software that let people communicate. These include:

  • Email and instant messaging
  • Wikis
  • Collaboration and social business platforms
    • Virtual meeting systems, like Zoom
    • Google Drive, Docs, Calendar (G Suite)
    • Enterprise social networking tools (maybe it just has employees, vendors, partners)

Levels of a firm
...

Before exploring the types of information systems, we should first talk about the levels of a firm so we know what level each system is targeted at.

Business organizations are hierarchies that tend to consist of three principal levels: senior management, middle management, and operational management. Information systems serve each level. Generally, middle management consists of scientists and knowledge workers; operational management consists of production and service workers, as well as dealt autoworkers. Senior management tends to be more concerned where higher-level decision making at the company.

Transaction processing system
...

One example is a transaction processing system, or a TPS. These serve operational managers and staff, performing and recording daily routine transactions that are necessary to conduct business. These include order entry, payroll, and shipping. This serves predefined, structured goals and decision making.

Business intelligence systems
...

Business intelligence systems, or BI systems, entail business intelligence – which is information that has been gathered from multiple sources, including external sources.

A BI system has the ability to bring information together from multiple places and allow us to perform analysis on that information. There are three major types:

  • Management information system, or MIS
  • Decision support system, or DSS
  • Executive support system, or ESS

A management information system is a piece of software that's used at the middle management level. Instead of people who are doing day-to-day stuff, these are people who are at a higher-level role who are involved in the Dayile decisions of an organization. These tend to provide reports on the firm's current performance, based on the data from various TPSs. That is – it takes data from many systems and provides conclusions from just one system.

This provides answers to routine questions with a predefined procedure for answering them. These typically have little analytic capability, and they can't answer questions like "why have sales jumped" – just how data correlates to each other.

But how do MISs get their data from the organization's TPSs? Generally, TPSs are all connected to several databases, which serve as the sole source for the MIS to generate reports.

A decision support system also serves middle management. In contrast, it sports non-routine decision making, like answering "what is the impact on production schedule if December sales doubled?" They're used to determine and product impacts based on "what-if" scenarios. They may use external information as well as TPS/MIS data.

The last type of BI system is an executive support system, or ESS. This is used to support senior management, addressing non-routine decisions. These can include investment opportunities, deciding whether or not to open a new branch, investing in new machinery or another company that could be merged or acquired, creating new products, entering a new market, and so on.

This is likely to incorporate data about external events (like new tax laws or competitors) as well as summarized information from internal MISs and DSSs. This is where a company can no longer operate with just an extranet or intranet; maybe they have thousands of customers and hundreds of employees.

Enterprise applications
...

Enterprise means "big business". You have a lot of products and sales; you're not a firm with a few employees. You're a multi-million or billion dollar company.

But by the time you get to become an Amazon, it's likely that you will need something that goes well beyond what these systems can provide. An enterprise application is a system for linking the enterprise. These offer functional and cross-functional separate, executing business processes across the firm.

There are three major applications:

  • Customer relationship management (CRM) systems
  • Supply chain management (SCM) systems
  • Enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems

Customer relationship management systems, or CRM systems, provide information to coordinate all the business processes that deal with customers. This includes sales, marketing, and customer service (including complaints). Its main propose is to help firms identify, attract, and retain the most profitable customers.

Supply chain management (SCM) systems manage a firm's relationship with suppliers. You may have suppliers all over the world, so you need to share information about orders, production, inventory levels, and the delivery of products and services. The goal here is to get the right amount of products to a destination in the least amount of time and with the lowest cost.

Finally, an enterprise resource planning (ERP) system is the largest, most complex piece of software in the world today. Not every company needs it.

ERP systems are made up of several pieces of software, perhaps implemented across several departments. The difference between this and a TPS is that each of these pieces of software is specifically designed to talk to each other, having been made by the same company. It collects data from different firm functions and stores data in a signale central data repository.

It enables:

  • The coordination of daily activities
  • Efficient responses to customer orders (production, inventory)
  • Helps managers make decisions about daily operations and longer-term planning

Organizations using information systems
...

E-business is the use of digital technology and the internet to drive major business processes, which basically every company today does.

E-commerce is a subset of e-business. It entails buying and selling goods and services through the internet. Such companies have developed ways to purchase goods and services through a website, usually.

E-government is the use of internet technology to deliver information and services to citizens, employees, and businesses. For example, if you wanted to make a business, you'd have to previously go into the secretary of state's office and get set up with a bunch of other government entities. But now, if you want to make a legitimate business that files taxes, you could use online services and get things set up electronically in under an hour.

Many companies also are involved in social business, which is a company's use of social networking platforms (both internal and external). This brings people (customers) in, but it also engages employees and suppliers.

Systems for collaboration and teamwork
...

Enterprise social networking software capabilities tend to have the following features:

  • Profiles
  • Content sharing
  • Feeds and notifications
  • Groups and team workspaces
  • Permissions and privacy

The sole difference between enterprise social networking software and popular social networking services like Facebook is the audience; one is open to all, the other is open to just the members of an organization.

It's worth noting that there are physical business benefits to collaboration technology. Investments in celebration technology can bring organizational improvements, giving a high return on investment. Benefits include:

  • Productivity
  • Quality
  • Innovation
  • Customer service
  • Financial performance, like profitability, sales, and sales growth
