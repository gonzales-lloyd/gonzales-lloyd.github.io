Chapter 10

This lecture addresses part of the class project, which asks for a business and revenue model.

E-commerce is any use of the internet and web to transact businesses, whether that's simply handling purchases online or actually providing online services. It began in 1995 and has grown exponentially, with marked stability even in recessions. The e-commerce revolution, however, is still in its early stages.

There are eight unique features of the internet and web as a commercial medium:

  • Ubiquity
  • Global reach
  • Universal standards
  • Richness
  • Interactivity
  • Information density
  • Personalization/customization
  • Social technology

These are all features that you can't get by purchasing something over the phone or at a store.

Ubiquity
...

The fact is that you can access the internet wherever in the world you want. That is, the internet/web touchingly is available everywhere and at anytime. What we've seen is that the marketplace is no longer bound by temporal, geographic locations, and we instead have the "marketspace".

Global reach
...

The technology we have now reaches across national boundaries and around earth. We're no longer bound by cultural or national boundaries; we can actually make sales to people around the world. The marketspace includes potentially billions of consumers and millions of business worldwide, not just the people near you.

Universal standards
...

We have one set of technology standards through internet standards. This means that:
- Different types of computer systems easily communicate with one another. Imagine if PCs and Macs couldn't communicate with each other; you might need a PC to buy from one company and a Mac to buy from another. But instead, they can all communicate with each other. What does that give you?
- You have much lower market entry costs, which are the costs merchants must pay to bring their goods to market; you can make your things available for purchase much more easily. Before this, you had to get a physical store location, pay for rent and shelving, and go through so many more hoops – all of which costs a lot of money. But if all you have to do is build a website on a web server, that's only a hundred bucks over the thousands you needed in the past.
- The search costs of consumers, or the effort required to find suitable products, is also much lower. In the past, you might have had to drive to multiple store locations to compare prices and quality. But now, you can just Google the differences and features with just your internet connection.

Richness
...

The internet provides richness, which is the ability to develop a combined product all in one space, usually supporting video, audio, and text messages. This makes it possible to deliver rich messages with text, audio, and video simultaneously to large numbers of people in single marketing messages. In the past, you had to hope that the salesperson was good enough to pitch it to you, or hope that a person would actually walk into your store and decide they wanted it right then and there.

Interactivity
...

Interactivity is where the technology works through interaction with the user. That is to say, the consumer's experience dynamically adjusts to their individual wants and needs.

It also allows them to communicate with you, saying that "I like this, but ..." – maybe one salesperson saying this internally wouldn't do much, but the aggregation of information makes it possible to actually make these decisions.

Information density
...

Nowadays, companies and consumers alike have a much better idea of who's selling what and how much they're selling it for, as well as how much a product even cost to create. The total amount and quality of information available to all market participants has increased, leading to both greater price and cost transparency.

It's a lot harder for a company to say "it cost us $2, but we're selling you this for $100" – a competitor with this knowledge can sell it for $50 instead, and consumers can decide they don't like that markup.

Personalization/customization
...

Technology provides the modification of both messages and goods. You might have seen this through recommendation systems – "people also bought ..."

You can also send such messages to a whole group of people who bought a certain product. Personalized messages can be sent to individuals as well as groups, while products and services can be customized to individual preferences.

Social technology
...

Technology can promote user content generation and social networking. Once you have enough followers, you can start promoting deals and products that your followers might pass onto other people. This means that new social and business models enable user content creation and distribution.

That is to say, you can rely on catching the attention of a few people and hoping that things go viral, so that what you put out gets shared by your followers, and their followers' followers.

Effect of the internet on the marketplace
...

  • The internet reduces information asymmetry. This is an imbalance in information across the board; perhaps before, companies had 90% of the information on the marketplace, while individuals only had 10%. That's no longer the case.
  • Strong network effects. Maybe you saw jeans on somebody before, and that didn't have much of an effect. But online, you can share things that can lead to broad effects very quickly.
  • Disintermediation: The reduction of intermediary layers, like distributors and retailors, between the manufacturer and the customer reduces the final cost to the consumer:

Digital goods
...

There are a variety of digital goods that you can purchase – there are a variety of things that are fully intangible, digital items that can simply be delivered over the internet. That is, digital goods are things you can't physically hold, but can still download. These are goods that can be delivered over a digital network, such as music, software, movies, books, and so on.

The cost of producing the first unit is almost the entire cost of the product. If you wanted to sell music in the past, you would need to make an album, burn it onto CDs, package it up, and then send it to distributors and retailers. After you made the music, you incurred a lot of costs to put it in peoples' hands.

But digitally, the costs of delivery over the internet is pretty low. You just need a website, a way to list the digital goods, and some infrastructure to actually distribute them. Or you might using an existing digital marketplace, like iTunes, which still takes a cut of what a user pays – but it's much less than the whole process described above.

Record labels, publishers, and so on have had to change quite a bit as a result. Industries with digital goods have undergone and will continue to see revolutionary changes. Are people going to miss physical goods?

Types of e-commerce
...

There are three major types of e-commerce you should know about:

  • Business-to-consumer (B2C), where businesses sell directly to consumers. One example is Amazon.
  • Business-to-Business (B2B), where businesses sells primarily to businesses. One example is Intel, which tends to sell processors that other companies stick in their products.
  • Consumer-to-consumer (C2C), where consumers sell to other consumers. Examples are Etsy and eBay (note that Craigslist isn't a good example, since you can't actually use Craigslist to make transactions. They're more of a B2B, since they tend to sell businesses the ability to make posts on Craigslist.)

Why isn't C2B a thing, like a consumer-to-business? The reason is because you need to be registered as a business for legal and tax purposes; if they just bought something from somebody in a garage, they wouldn't have that paper trail. So something like a sole proprietorship is necessary.

Mobile e-commerce
...

We now have m-commerce, which is the use of wireless mobile devices for purchasing goods and services. You might be using your phone's browser, or you might even be using a mobile app to directly buy from a store. This is the fastest growing form of e-commerce, since there are more and more people getting devices and more and more companies building websites and apps that function on mobile devices.

There are now 5.3 billion mobile phone users worldwide, so it makes sense that companies want to engage with these users.

E-Commerce business models
...

This more closely addresses the project requirements.

There are seven types of e-commerce business models:

  • E-Tailer (Amazon)
  • Transaction broker (like an online stock broker facilitating the transaction on your behalf)
  • Market creator (eBay - "we don't sell a product, but provide you a platform for people to find your stuff and we take a cut of it")
  • Content provider (iTunes - "we give you the ability to sell your digital goods, post them on our platform, and we take a cut")
  • Community provider (Facebook- "we provide social networks that you can join, creating communities that people can interact with")
  • Portals (Google, Bing, Yahoo - primarily search engines, they create a way to find other things on the internet)
  • Service provider (online data storage - "you don't buy a digital or physical product, but you're just renting storage or services from us")

Generally, you'll have one business model, though it's possible you fall into one or more of these buckets depending on your business's complexity.

Revenue models
...

But you might have multiple revenue models. These are:

  • Advertising (how Facebook and Google tend to make money because of how massive their userbases are)
  • Sales (Sell something at a profit margin)
  • Subscription (For a certain amount of money over some period of time, I'll give you a service or product. You're looking for volume; if you have a million people paying $5 a month, that's a lot of money coming in.)
  • Free/freemium (You can get most of these things for free, but you can also pay more money for other things)
  • Transaction fees (Sell stuff that you don't make a lot of money off of directly, but also get some transaction fees off of it.)
  • Affiliate (Get a cut from people who sell your products and earn some revenue themselves.)

You may have multiple of these. You might sell a product, but you might also plan to get a community together over a few years and make money off of advertising.

Building a e-commerce website
...

It takes a few things to build a site:

  • You need a team with the skills required to make decisions about:
    • Technology (databases, backends, frontends, etc.)
    • Site design (font faces, visual design, etc.)
    • Social and information policies (what information do we want to collect about our users?)
    • Hardware, software, and telecommunications infrastructure

In that respect, you have a few options for building a website:

  • Completely in-house
  • Mixed responsibility
  • Completely outsourced

What you chose depends on your website budget. Not everybody is Amazon, so a website might cost you anywhere from a few thousand dollars to create to millions of dollars per year. 50% of the budget tends to lie in system maintenance and content creation.
